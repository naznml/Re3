Мәтін Текст

B1+ Reading Practice · Geography and Climate

Topic: The Geography and Climate of Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan is the largest country in Central Asia and one of the largest countries in the world. It is landlocked, which means that it has no direct access to the ocean. Kazakhstan borders Russia, China, Kyrgyzstan, Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan. The Caspian Sea lies to the west, but it is an inland body of water rather than an open sea.

The country’s size strongly influences its geography. Kazakhstan has wide plains, dry steppes, deserts and mountain areas. Much of the central and western part of the country is open and dry, while the east and southeast contain higher mountains. These different landscapes affect where people live, how they travel and what kinds of economic activities are possible.

Kazakhstan has a sharply continental climate. Summers are often hot, especially in the south and in lowland areas. Winters can be very cold, particularly in the north and on the open steppe. In some regions, strong winds and snow make winter travel difficult.

Rainfall is limited in many parts of the country. Some mountain areas receive more precipitation, but dry regions depend on rivers, lakes and irrigation systems. Because water is not equally available everywhere, it is important for agriculture and settlement.

Geography and climate have shaped Kazakhstan’s economy and way of life. People need strong heating systems in winter, while summer heat can affect farming and water use. At the same time, the steppe, mountains and open landscapes are an important part of the country’s natural identity.

Мәтін Текст

B1+ Reading Practice · Geography and Climate

Topic: The Geography and Climate of the United Kingdom

The United Kingdom is an island country in north-western Europe. It is made up of four parts: England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. Great Britain includes England, Scotland and Wales, while Northern Ireland is located on the island of Ireland. Because the UK is surrounded by the Atlantic Ocean, the North Sea and the Irish Sea, the sea strongly influences its climate and daily life.

The UK’s landscape is varied, although the country is much smaller than Kazakhstan. Scotland and Wales have many hills and mountains, while large parts of England are lower and flatter. Northern Ireland has green countryside, lakes and coastal areas. Rivers are important for cities and transport because many towns developed near water.

The UK has a temperate maritime climate. Winters are usually milder than in many continental countries, and summers are generally not extremely hot. However, the weather can change quickly. A sunny morning may be followed by rain, wind or cloud in the afternoon.

Rainfall is common, but it is not the same everywhere. Western areas, especially parts of Scotland, Wales and north-west England, are often wetter because they face moist air from the Atlantic. Eastern and south-eastern areas are usually drier.

The UK’s climate affects its lifestyle and culture. People often talk about the weather because it changes frequently and influences travel, outdoor activities and farming. The mild and wet climate also helps create green landscapes, which are an important part of the country’s image.

Мәтін Текст

B1+ Reading Practice · History, Capital and Main Cities

Topic: Kazakhstan: From History to a Modern Capital

Kazakhstan’s modern history is closely connected with the growth of its cities. For many years, Almaty was the country’s most important city and served as the capital. It was known as Alma-Ata during the Soviet period and became Almaty after Kazakhstan gained independence in 1991. Although it is no longer the seat of government, Almaty remains a major cultural, educational and commercial centre.

After independence, Kazakhstan needed a capital that could support the development of the whole country. In 1994, the government decided to move the capital from Almaty to Aqmola, a city in the north-central part of Kazakhstan. The transfer was completed in 1997, and in 1998 the city was renamed Astana, which means “capital”.

Astana was chosen partly because of its central location and its potential for future growth. The move was also intended to spread development beyond the southeast of the country. Since then, Astana has become a symbol of modern Kazakhstan. New government buildings, wide avenues and cultural centres have changed the city’s appearance.

The city has had several names in its history. It was once called Akmolinsk, later Tselinograd, then Aqmola. It became Astana in 1998, Nur-Sultan in 2019, and Astana again in 2022. These name changes reflect different periods in the country’s political and social history.

Today, Kazakhstan’s main cities have different roles. Astana is the political capital, Almaty is a cultural and business centre, and other cities such as Shymkent, Karaganda and Aktobe are important for population, industry and regional life. Together, they show how Kazakhstan combines history, independence and modern development.

B1+ Reading Practice · History, Capital and Main Cities

Topic: London and the Historical Cities of the United Kingdom

The history of the United Kingdom can be seen clearly in its cities. London, the capital, is one of the world’s major financial, commercial and cultural centres. Its history goes back to Roman times, when it was known as Londinium. The city developed near the River Thames, which helped trade, transport and communication.

London became important not only because of trade, but also because of political power. Westminster became the centre of government and royal authority, while the City of London remained connected with business and finance. This combination helped London grow into a national and international centre.

The United Kingdom is made up of England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, and each part has important cities. Edinburgh, the capital of Scotland, is known for its historic buildings, universities and cultural festivals. Cardiff, the capital of Wales, developed as an important port and later became a centre of Welsh politics and culture. Belfast, the capital of Northern Ireland, became known for shipbuilding and industry.

Other British cities also have strong historical identities. Manchester and Birmingham grew rapidly during the Industrial Revolution and became important for manufacturing and trade. Liverpool became famous as a port city, connecting Britain with different parts of the world.

These cities show that the UK’s history is not limited to one place. London is the political and cultural capital, but other cities also helped shape the country’s economy, industry, education and identity. Understanding the UK means looking at both its capital and its regional cities.

B1 Reading Practice · Political System and Economy

Topic: How Global Trade Shapes a Country’s Economy

Global trade means buying and selling goods and services between countries. It can help a country’s economy grow because businesses get access to more customers and consumers get more choice. Trade also allows countries to focus on the products they can make well.

One important idea in trade is specialisation. Some countries have natural resources, good farmland, advanced technology or skilled workers. For example, one country may export grain or oil, while another may export machines or digital services. When countries focus on their strengths, they can become more productive.

Trade also creates competition. Local companies may need to improve quality, lower prices or use new technology when they compete with foreign businesses. This can be good for consumers and can make companies more modern.

However, trade can also create problems. Some local industries may suffer if cheaper imported goods become popular. Workers may need new skills when old jobs disappear. Small businesses may also find it difficult to sell products abroad because of transport costs, rules or language barriers.

For this reason, governments need careful trade policies. Trade can support growth, but countries also need to protect workers, support local businesses and prepare people for economic change.

B1 Reading Practice · Political System and Economy

Topic: How a Parliamentary System Works

A parliamentary system is a form of government in which parliament plays a central role. People vote in elections to choose representatives. These representatives discuss national problems, make laws and decide how public money should be used. In this system, government is expected to explain its decisions to parliament and to the public.

In many parliamentary countries, the political party that wins the most seats forms the government. Its leader often becomes the prime minister. The prime minister works with other ministers to manage areas such as education, health, transport and the economy.

The opposition is also important. Opposition parties do not usually run the government, but they question decisions, suggest other ideas and speak for citizens who support different policies. This helps keep political debate open.

A parliamentary system works well when representatives listen to people, laws are discussed openly and citizens take part in elections. It does not mean that every problem is solved quickly. However, it gives people a way to influence political decisions through voting, public discussion and democratic institutions.

Topic: Traditions and Identity in a Changing World

Traditions help people feel connected to their family, community and country. They can include food, clothing, ceremonies, music, festivals and ways of showing respect. For many people, traditions are not only old customs; they are part of national identity.

However, modern life is changing the way people practise traditions. Globalisation has made it easier for people to travel, study abroad, watch foreign films and use international social media. Music and dance can also be performed on modern stages or promoted through the internet.

The main challenge is balance. If traditions are kept only as entertainment, their deeper meaning may be lost. But if people understand why a custom is important, they can preserve it in a modern form. In this way, traditions can continue to support identity while still allowing society to change.

This can be a strength because it allows them to communicate with different communities and take part in global education, business and culture. Schools, families and media can help preserve language. Children learn not only grammar and vocabulary, but also how language is used in real life. Traditional songs, poems, films and books can make a language feel alive and meaningful. Digital tools can also help, especially when young people use apps, videos and online platforms in their own language.

However, language preservation should not mean rejecting other languages. In the modern world, people often need international languages for study and work. The challenge is to find balance: learning global languages while continuing to respect and use one’s own language. When this balance is kept, language can connect people to both their cultural roots and the wider world.

B1 Reading Practice · Language, Culture and Art

Topic: Art, Music and Culture in Modern Society

Art and music are important parts of cultural life. They help people express emotions, remember history and share ideas. A painting, a song, a dance or a film can show what a society values and how people understand the world around them.

Traditional art forms often carry cultural memory. Musical instruments, folk dances, national costumes and old stories can connect people with the past. They are especially important during festivals, ceremonies and public celebrations. These events allow communities to show their identity and pass cultural knowledge to younger generations.

At the same time, art is always changing. Modern artists often combine traditional themes with new styles, technology and global influences. A musician may use a traditional instrument in a modern song. A film director may tell an old story in a new visual style. This can help culture reach younger audiences and become more visible internationally.

Some people worry that global culture can make local art less important. Popular films, music and online trends may attract more attention than traditional forms. However, technology can also protect culture. Museums, online archives, music platforms and social media can help people discover local art and share it with the world.

For this reason, art and music should not be seen only as entertainment. They are also connected with identity, education and cultural preservation. A society that supports its artists also supports its memory and future.
